## A. Signature Sentence

> "We can know more than we can tell."

This is the most-quoted sentence in Polanyi's corpus. Its power comes from:
- **Rhythm**: Eight monosyllables. Clean, memorable, quotable.
- **Paradox**: It states something that sounds impossible — knowing more than you can communicate — and makes it feel obviously true once stated.
- **Immediacy**: No technical vocabulary. Any reader understands the words; the surprise is in the combination.
- **Self-reference**: The sentence itself performs what it describes — it points to something that exceeds its own explicit content.

Variants in the corpus:
- "I shall reconsider human knowledge by starting from the fact that we can know more than we can tell." (Opening of The Tacit Dimension)
- "The skill of a driver cannot be replaced by a thorough schooling in the theory of the motorcar."
- "We know a person's face but cannot usually tell how we recognize it."

## B. Rhetorical Opening Pattern

Almost every chapter, lecture, and major essay begins with a concrete, accessible physical example:

| Work | Opening Example |
|------|----------------|
| The Tacit Dimension, Ch. 1 | A man can recognize a face in a crowd of a million — but cannot describe how. |
| Personal Knowledge, Ch. 1 | The Copernican revolution was accepted not because it fit the data better, but because it felt more beautiful. |
| "Republic of Science" | Science resembles a jigsaw puzzle: no single solver can see the whole picture; each adjusts to the others. |
| Science, Faith and Society | Science depends on the freedom to pursue questions regardless of social utility. |
| "Life's Irreducible Structure" | A machine is built according to a blueprint. An organism grows according to a plan that is itself a product of the organism. |

Pattern: **Concrete → Surprising → Abstract → Philosophical**

He never begins with a thesis. He begins with a scene that makes the reader feel the puzzle before they understand it.

## C. Recurring Concrete Examples

These examples appear across multiple works, refined and deepened with each use:

### The Bicycle
- **Uses**: The Tacit Dimension (most fully), Personal Knowledge, lectures.
- **Function**: Shows that practical skill cannot be reduced to explicit rules.
- **Full version**: "The rule observed by a bicyclist when keeping his balance is this: When he starts falling to the right he turns the wheel to the right, so that the course of the bicycle is deflected on a curve towards the right. This curve makes him fall to the left so he now turns the wheel to the left to deflect him on a curve to the left. By repeating this series of alternating turns, each time with an angular velocity proportional to the square of the velocity divided by the radius of the curve, he keeps upright."
- **Punch line**: "No one, I believe, ever learns to ride a bicycle by reading such a rule."

### The Surgeon
- **Uses**: Personal Knowledge, The Tacit Dimension, lectures.
- **Function**: Shows tacit knowing in professional expertise.
- **Version**: "The surgeon who cuts and ties arteries cannot state all the rules he follows. If he were to interrupt the operation and try to state them fully, he would be unable to do so; and even if he could, following those explicit rules would likely produce a worse surgical result than trusting his trained judgment."

### Face Recognition
- **Uses**: Personal Knowledge, The Tacit Dimension (opening pages).
- **Function**: Shows how we integrate subsidiary clues into focal recognition without being able to say how.
- **Version**: "We can recognize the face of a person in a crowd of a million. We are picking up thousands of micro-features — the set of the jaw, the depth of the eyes — and integrating them into recognition. Ask us how we recognize that face, and we cannot say."

### The Chess Prodigy
- **Uses**: Personal Knowledge, lectures.
- **Function**: Shows how expertise exceeds explicit knowledge.
- **Version**: The chess master sees the board as a whole — sees threats and opportunities simultaneously. The novice sees the pieces. The difference is not more calculation; it is a different kind of seeing.

### The Semmelweis Case
- **Uses**: Personal Knowledge.
- **Function**: Shows how scientific communities can resist valid knowledge because it violates existing frameworks.
- **Polanyi's point**: Semmelweis's data were correct, but his theory violated the tacit framework of Viennese medicine. Rejection was not irrational — it was the normal operation of a paradigm maintaining itself.

## D. Syntactic Patterns

### 1. The "We cannot… but we can…" Structure
Establishes a gap between explicit and tacit knowledge, then crosses it:
- "We cannot specify what clues we rely on; but we can recognize the face."
- "We cannot state the rules; but we can perform the skill."
- "We cannot prove our premises; but we can commit ourselves to them responsibly."

### 2. The "This is not… it is…" Correction
Polanyi frequently corrects a natural but mistaken description:
- "This is not a matter of personal bias. It is the form that responsible knowledge takes."
- "This is not mysticism. It is the structure of all knowing."
- "This is not subjectivism. The personal element in knowledge is not the same as the arbitrary."

### 3. The Long Qualifying Clause Followed by Short Declaration
Builds complexity, then lands decisively:
- "Although science depends on tradition, although it requires submission to authority, although it cannot prove its own premises — it is still our highest form of knowledge."

### 4. First-Person Self-Implication
Unlike most academic philosophers, Polanyi includes himself in the phenomenon:
- "I, too, cannot tell how I came to believe that the earth is round."
- "I shall reconsider human knowledge by starting from the fact that we can know more than we can tell."
- "I maintain that science cannot exist without the personal commitment of those who practice it."

## E. Vocabulary Profile

### Terms He Coins or Redefines
- **Tacit**: Used with philosophical precision to mean "known but not fully articulable." Not mystical; not unconscious.
- **Subsidiary / Focal**: Borrowed from psychology, elevated to epistemology. "Subsidiary awareness" = awareness of the clues; "focal awareness" = awareness of the target.
- **Indwelling**: To know by inhabiting a body of practice or belief, the way we inhabit language. Not passive — it requires active participation.
- **Fiduciary**: From the Latin *fidere* (to trust). Knowledge is fiduciary when it is held as a responsible commitment. Polanyi uses this to distinguish his view from both relativism (arbitrary commitment) and Cartesian foundationalism (no commitment needed).
- **Personal**: Used technically, not colloquially. "Personal" does not mean subjective or arbitrary — it means that a person is necessarily involved.
- **Post-critical**: After the Cartesian program of eliminating all unproven beliefs. Not anti-critical; not a return to pre-Enlightenment dogma.

### Terms He Consistently Avoids or Criticizes
- **Objective** (in the positivist sense): Polanyi does not abandon objectivity, but he redefines it. He uses "universal" or "valid" instead when he means "not arbitrary."
- **Data**: He tends to say "clues" or "evidence" to emphasize their interpretive character.
- **Proof**: He rarely claims to prove anything. He "maintains," "argues," "believes."

## F. Argumentative Move: The Historical Case Study

Polanyi treats history of science as philosophy:
- He does not use thought experiments (unlike Descartes, Rawls).
- He uses real episodes: Copernicus, Lavoisier, Semmelweis, Pasteur, his own adsorption theory.
- Pattern: describe the episode → show that the official (positivist) account fails to explain it → show that his account (tacit knowing, fiduciary commitment) explains it naturally.
- This makes his philosophy feel grounded and honest — he is not constructing a convenient example; he is explaining actual history.

## G. Tone Profile

| Dimension | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Register | High but not remote — formal diction, conversational rhythm |
| Confidence | High. He states his positions clearly. Rarely hedges with "perhaps" or "it might be argued." |
| Humility | Present but focused: he acknowledges mystery in knowing, not uncertainty about his own account |
| Polemicism | Moderate. He attacks positivism and Cartesian skepticism, but rarely personally. |
| Self-disclosure | High for an academic philosopher. He uses "I believe," "I maintain," "I hold." |
| Patience with difficulty | High. He will spend pages developing a single example, returning to it from different angles. |

## H. Negative Space: What He Does Not Do

- **He does not use formal logic or symbolic notation**: Unlike Frege, Russell, or Carnap, Polanyi makes no use of formal systems. This is deliberate — tacit knowing resists formalization.
- **He does not do linguistic analysis**: Despite writing in the era of Oxford ordinary language philosophy, he does not analyze "knowing," "believing," etc. as linguistic items. He analyzes knowing as a phenomenon.
- **He does not appeal to consensus**: He does not use "most philosophers think" or "it is generally agreed" as arguments. He starts from observation, not from authority.
- **He does not compartmentalize**: He refuses to separate epistemology from philosophy of science from political philosophy from philosophy of religion. His work is deliberately unified around a single central claim.

## I. The Characteristic Essay Structure

1. **Scene/Example**: A concrete case that generates the puzzle.
2. **Official Account**: The standard (positivist, rationalist) explanation of the case.
3. **Failure of the Official Account**: Why the standard explanation fails — usually because it ignores the personal/tacit element.
4. **Polanyi's Account**: How tacit knowing and personal commitment explain what the official account could not.
5. **Generalization**: Showing that this is not a special case — the same structure appears across science, skill, and society.
6. **Implication**: What follows for philosophy, science policy, or human self-understanding.

---

## J. Compression and Density

- Personal Knowledge is dense: 428 pages, but each chapter carries genuine weight.
- The Tacit Dimension is deliberately compressed: he describes the compression strategy in the preface: "I have tried to state my conclusions without the supporting detail that Personal Knowledge provides."
- His essays are more accessible because the compression is extreme: "The Republic of Science" makes a complex argument in 20 pages; a philosopher of science would take a monograph.
- He is not verbose, but he is not minimalist either. He gives examples until the concept is clear, then stops.
