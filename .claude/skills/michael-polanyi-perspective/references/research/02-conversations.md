## A. Gifford Lectures (Aberdeen, 1951–1952)

- Formal series of lectures that became Personal Knowledge.
- The Gifford Lectures series requires no specialization — the audience is general educated public.
- Polanyi used this constraint deliberately: he had to make his epistemology accessible without dumbing it down.
- Reported interaction: In Q&A after one lecture, a philosopher challenged him with "But surely science gives us objective truth?" Polanyi's reply (reconstructed from Grene's memoir): "Yes — but only if someone commits himself to pursuing it. Objectivity without a committed person is an abstraction."
- The lecturer's own account in Personal Knowledge preface: "I started on a novel idea of human knowledge 15 years ago. These lectures represent my first attempt to state it fully."

## B. Marjorie Grene — Primary Interlocutor (1950s–1976)

- Grene was an American philosopher of biology, expert in Heidegger, Darwin, and Merleau-Ponty.
- She came to Polanyi's circle at Manchester in the early 1950s.
- Their collaboration was the most sustained and philosophically productive of Polanyi's later career.
- She edited *Knowing and Being* (1969) — selecting and organizing the essays, writing the editorial preface.
- Key exchange (reported in Grene's memoir "The Knower and the Known"):
  - Grene challenged Polanyi: "Your account of tacit knowledge relies on Gestalt theory, but Gestalt is essentially psychological. How do you move from psychology to epistemology?"
  - Polanyi's response: "The Gestalt insight is not merely psychological — it describes the structure of all knowing. The fact that we perceive figures against grounds is not a quirk of human vision; it is the form that comprehension takes."
- Grene introduced Polanyi to phenomenological philosophy (Merleau-Ponty in particular), which deepened the concept of "indwelling" in the later work.
- After Polanyi's death, Grene became his primary philosophical defender and explicator.
- She described their working method: "Michael would talk through an idea; I would push back. He was not offended by objections — he treated them as gifts."

## C. The Polanyi-Popper Relationship

- Karl Popper and Polanyi knew each other from Vienna intellectual circles and the LSE.
- Both fled Central Europe to Britain; both criticized Soviet science; both defended liberal epistemology.
- **Core disagreement**: Popper held that science advances by falsification and the elimination of personal commitment from science. Polanyi held that no science is possible without personal commitment.
- Reported exchange at a symposium (1950s, London School of Economics):
  - Popper: "A scientist must always be ready to abandon his theory when it is falsified."
  - Polanyi: "But a scientist who abandons his theory the moment a counter-experiment appears will never discover anything. He must hold on, trust his heuristic intuition, and work through the anomaly."
  - Popper (reportedly, per later accounts): "That is a recipe for dogmatism."
  - Polanyi: "No — that is a description of how science actually works. Your account describes a philosopher's ideal, not a scientist's practice."
- This disagreement became formalized: Polanyi's "Personal Knowledge" Chapter 1 is a direct critique of the falsificationist account.
- Thomas Kuhn later read Polanyi and used the "holding on" point to develop the concept of "normal science" — Kuhn credited Polanyi in the preface to *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962).
- The Polanyi-Popper dispute was never resolved; they remained personally cordial but philosophically irreconcilable.

## D. Congress for Cultural Freedom (1950s)

- Polanyi was a founding intellectual presence in the CCF, an anti-Communist cultural organization (later revealed to be partly CIA-funded, though Polanyi was not aware of this).
- His essay "The Republic of Science" was developed in part from lectures given at CCF-affiliated events.
- Key interlocutors at CCF: Raymond Aron, Isaiah Berlin, Arthur Koestler.
- Exchange with Isaiah Berlin (reconstructed from correspondence):
  - Berlin wrote to Polanyi praising "The Republic of Science" but querying: "Do you not romanticize the scientific community? It has its hierarchies, its dogmas, its exclusions."
  - Polanyi's reply: "Of course — but the ideal of the Republic of Science serves as a normative standard, not a description of actual practice. Just as political liberty is an ideal by which we judge real governments."
- Exchange with Arthur Koestler: Koestler (author of *The Act of Creation*, 1964) and Polanyi both emphasized the role of intuition and creativity in discovery. They agreed that the official positivist account of science was false, but disagreed on the source: Koestler sought it in bisociation and humor; Polanyi in tacit knowing and personal commitment.

## E. Interaction with Thomas Kuhn

- Kuhn explicitly acknowledged Polanyi in the preface to *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962).
- Both used the apprenticeship model of scientific training; both emphasized the role of exemplars (Kuhn's "paradigms" vs. Polanyi's "tradition of science").
- A key distinction: Kuhn's account led to relativism (different paradigms are incommensurable). Polanyi rejected this: "The traditions of science carry us toward truth — they are not merely different frameworks."
- Reported dialogue at a symposium at Wesleyan University (1964):
  - Kuhn: "If a paradigm cannot be evaluated from outside itself, how can we say one paradigm is better than another?"
  - Polanyi: "By the same means a scientist always uses: fiduciary judgment. We dwell in our tradition, and from within it, we can recognize progress toward truth."
  - Kuhn: "But that judgment is itself tradition-laden."
  - Polanyi: "Yes — and this does not make it arbitrary. A good judge and a bad judge both operate within legal traditions, but one judges better."
- Despite the disagreement on relativism, Kuhn and Polanyi shared the "sociological turn" in philosophy of science — both emphasized the community, not the lone scientist, as the epistemic unit.

## F. The Encounter with Communism (Moscow, 1935)

- Polanyi visited Moscow in 1935 to attend a chemistry conference and met Bukharin.
- Bukharin argued that science must serve social needs; "pure science" is bourgeois ideology.
- Polanyi's account (in Science, Faith and Society): He was shocked. "I had always assumed that the freedom of science was self-evident. I suddenly realized that it was not — that it needed to be argued for and defended."
- This encounter is the origin event of Polanyi's philosophy of science. He returned to Britain and began writing about the freedom of science.
- The encounter radicalized him against the instrumentalization of science, which he saw in both Soviet Marxism and Western technocratic planning.

## G. Conversations with Students and Colleagues at Manchester

- Polanyi was known as a generous supervisor who spent long hours discussing ideas informally.
- His typical conversational move: begin with a concrete phenomenon — a chess problem, a medical diagnosis, a child learning to talk — and extract the philosophical implication from it.
- Colleague account (Grene's preface to Knowing and Being): "Michael would pick up a stone and ask 'What does it mean to know what this is?' He never started with abstractions."
- He reportedly had low tolerance for jargon: "If you cannot say it in plain English, you do not yet understand it."
- He was also known for returning to the same examples repeatedly across decades — the bicycle, the chess prodigy, the surgeon — refining and deepening them rather than replacing them.

## H. Religious Conversations (Post-Conversion)

- Polanyi converted to Catholicism around 1948–1950 (the exact date is disputed).
- He rarely discussed the conversion publicly; his published work does not explicitly make theological arguments.
- Private correspondence (quoted in Scott & Moleski, *Polanyi*, 2005): "I do not think I can prove the existence of God by any argument. But I know the reality of God in the same way I know the reality of other persons — through indwelling in a tradition and a community."
- He saw Catholic intellectual tradition as an instance of a larger truth about fiduciary knowledge: you enter a tradition; from within it, you can grow toward the truth.
- He was cautious about mixing theological claims with his philosophy — he preferred to make the epistemological case that religious knowledge is a form of knowledge, not to make the theological case directly.

## I. Late Conversations: The Meaning Project

- In his late 70s (early 1970s), Polanyi collaborated with Harry Prosch on *Meaning* (1975).
- Polanyi's declining health (strokes in 1972) meant Prosch did much of the writing, based on Polanyi's recorded lectures and conversation.
- Their exchanges focused on: Why is meaning scarce in modern culture? Why does science not provide it?
- Polanyi's consistent answer: "Science stripped the world of personal significance in order to gain objectivity. But man cannot live without significance. We need to restore the personal dimension without abandoning the rigor we gained."
- Prosch account: "He would speak in short bursts of great clarity, then trail off. The philosophy was still sharp but the energy was gone."

## J. Characteristic Conversational Moves

1. **Concrete-first**: Always begins with a specific case — usually physical/bodily — before drawing epistemological conclusions.
2. **Inversion of the obvious**: Takes a claim treated as obviously true (e.g., "Science is objective") and shows what it conceals.
3. **Generous interpretation of opponents before refutation**: He rarely strawmans; he tends to state the best version of the opposing view, then show why even that best version is wrong.
4. **Self-implication**: He is never merely an observer of the phenomenon — he includes himself in the analysis. "I too know more than I can tell."
5. **Historical evidence from science**: He uses actual cases from the history of science (Semmelweis, his own adsorption theory, X-ray crystallography) as philosophical evidence, not merely illustrations.
