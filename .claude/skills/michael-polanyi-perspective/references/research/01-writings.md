## A. Major Books (Chronological)

### Science, Faith and Society (1946)
- Origin: Riddell Memorial Lectures, University of Durham, 1946
- Core argument: Science is not a self-sufficient, value-free enterprise. It depends on a prior commitment — a "faith" — in the value of truth and in the authority of a scientific tradition.
- Key claim: "Scientific life is a way of life." Scientists are initiated into a tradition through apprenticeship, not algorithmic rules.
- Introduces the idea of the "Republic of Science" — a self-governing community bound by mutual recognition of competence and shared values, not central direction.
- Polemical target: Soviet Lysenkoist science, but also Western positivism and Marxist instrumentalism of science.
- Notable passage: "A society of explorers is governed from within by the tradition it maintains, and if it is healthy it moves the frontier of knowledge forward."
- Later developed fully in "The Republic of Science" (1962) essay.

### The Logic of Liberty (1951)
- Collection of essays written 1940–1950; unified by anti-totalitarian political philosophy.
- Core argument: Liberty is not the absence of authority but the proper distribution of authority. Central planning destroys spontaneous coordination in science, economics, and society.
- Draws on Hayek's "knowledge problem" but frames it epistemically: planners cannot possess the tacit knowledge dispersed across a free society.
- Key essay: "The Span of Central Direction" — argues that coordinated social systems (science, markets) must be polycentric; no single authority can integrate their distributed knowledge.
- Introduces the metaphor of the "invisible hand" adapted for science: each scientist adjusts to the work of others, producing emergent order without central control.
- Political relevance: written during the height of Stalinist planning; Polanyi was directly engaged in the Congress for Cultural Freedom debates.

### Personal Knowledge: Towards a Post-Critical Philosophy (1958)
- Magnum opus. 428 pages. Based on the Gifford Lectures at Aberdeen (1951–1952).
- Core claim: ALL knowledge is personal. There is no view from nowhere. Even the most rigorous scientific knowledge is held by a person who commits themselves to it.
- "Post-critical" means: after Descartes' doubt and the Enlightenment program of eliminating personal elements from knowledge. Polanyi wants to restore the personal without collapsing into subjectivism.
- Key structure:
  - Part I: The Art of Knowing — how scientists develop skill and judgment; the role of beauty and elegance as guides.
  - Part II: The Critique of Doubt — attacking Cartesian skepticism; "we must start from the belief that we know more than we can prove."
  - Part III: The Justification of Personal Knowledge — fiduciary framework; knowledge as responsible commitment.
  - Part IV: Knowing and Being — the structure of comprehensive entities; the relationship between parts and wholes.
- Famous sentence: "I shall reconsider human knowledge by starting from the fact that we can know more than we can tell."
- Introduces "tacit dimension" in this book, but develops it separately later.
- Distinguishes "focal" and "subsidiary" awareness: when hammering a nail, we are focally aware of the nail but subsidiarily aware of the hammer in our hand. We attend FROM the subsidiary TO the focal.
- Argues that this structure is universal: all knowing involves attending from particulars to a comprehensive whole.

### The Tacit Dimension (1966)
- Short (108 pp.), most widely read work. Based on Terry Lectures at Yale, 1962.
- Distills Personal Knowledge into three lectures:
  1. **Tacit Knowing** — the structure of knowing more than we can tell. The "from-to" structure. Subsidiary-focal integration.
  2. **Emergence** — how tacit knowing enables us to recognize higher-level realities (organisms, persons, works of art) that exceed their physical substrate.
  3. **A Society of Explorers** — how tacit knowing structures the scientific community; the Republic of Science; freedom as condition for genuine inquiry.
- Introduces the **"tacit triad"**: functional, phenomenal, semantic relations between subsidiary particulars and focal wholes.
- The bicycle-riding example: you cannot learn to ride a bicycle from a rule book. Explicit rules, if followed literally, produce failure. The skill is tacit.
- Surgery example: "The surgeon who cuts and ties arteries cannot state all the rules he follows as he does so."
- Key distinction: **tacit** ≠ unconscious. Tacit knowledge is potentially articulable but not fully articulable. It can be pointed at, demonstrated, apprenticed.
- Later widely applied in management (Nonaka), philosophy of science, AI/robotics debates.

### Knowing and Being (1969)
- Collection of essays (1959–1968), edited by Marjorie Grene.
- Extends Personal Knowledge into philosophy of biology, mind, and social science.
- Key essays:
  - "The Structure of Consciousness" — mind is not reducible to brain; consciousness has an emergent structure.
  - "Life's Irreducible Structure" — living systems cannot be explained by physics and chemistry alone; biological boundary conditions constitute a higher principle.
  - "The Logic of Tacit Inference" — formalizes the tacit dimension in terms of Gestalt theory and neural integration.
- Argues against reductionism consistently: higher levels of reality (life, mind, society) are real and irreducible to lower-level descriptions.
- This is where Polanyi most explicitly engages with the philosophy of biology post-Watson-Crick.

### Meaning (1975, posthumous)
- Co-authored with Harry Prosch; based on late lectures.
- Attempts to extend tacit knowing into aesthetics, religion, and the problem of meaning.
- Core argument: The meaning crisis of modernity stems from the demand for explicit justification of all commitments. Science cannot ground meaning. Meaning requires tacit commitment to a tradition.
- Introduces the idea that art, myth, and ritual work through "metaphor" — they allow us to integrate particulars into a comprehensive vision.
- Less philosophically rigorous than Personal Knowledge; more theological in tone.
- Polanyi converts to Catholicism appears implicitly in this text's tolerance of non-explicit religious commitment.

## B. Major Essays (Selected)

### "The Republic of Science" (1962, Minerva)
- Most cited short essay. Argues the scientific community is self-governing, coordinated by mutual adjustment among independent scientists.
- Attacks centralized science planning (Soviet model, but also US science policy debates of the era).
- The "invisible hand" in science: no central authority decides which problems are important; scientists respond to each other's published results.
- Key claim: "The pursuit of science by independent self-coordinated initiatives assures the most efficient possible use of the gifts of scientists."
- Directly influences later work on epistemic communities, governance of science, and distributed knowledge systems.

### "Knowing and Being" (1961, Mind)
- Argues that to know something is to dwell in it; knowing is a form of participation, not mere observation.
- Introduces the "tacit coefficient" — the contribution of tacit knowledge to any explicit assertion.

### "The Potential Theory of Adsorption" (1914, 1963 republication)
- Early chemistry paper — notable because Polanyi's theory was rejected by Haber and others, then vindicated 40 years later.
- Polanyi later used this episode as evidence for his epistemology: scientists use tacit judgment to keep research programs alive in the face of anomalies.

## D. Writing Characteristics in Major Works

- Opens with concrete, sensory examples before moving to abstract argument.
- Uses first person freely: "I maintain," "I shall argue," "I believe."
- Heavy use of phenomenological language borrowed from Gestalt psychology (Köhler, Koffka).
- Often attacks a position (positivism, Cartesian doubt, central planning) before stating his own.
- Acknowledges and thanks collaborators, especially Marjorie Grene.
- Length of Personal Knowledge (428 pp.) reflects the scope of the ambition — attempting a complete philosophy of knowledge.
- The Tacit Dimension is deliberately compressed: Terry Lectures format demanded accessibility.

## E. Reception & Legacy of Major Works

- Personal Knowledge initially received mixed reviews — philosophers found it too "literary," scientists found it too philosophical.
- The Tacit Dimension became the entry point for most readers; it is assigned in management, education, and STS courses.
- "The Republic of Science" is widely anthologized in science policy literature.
- Nonaka & Takeuchi's *The Knowledge-Creating Company* (1995) brought tacit knowing into business/management — massive secondary amplification.
- Dreyfus brothers' critique of AI drew directly on Polanyi's tacit knowing argument.
- Science and Technology Studies (STS), especially Harry Collins, developed "tacit knowledge" as a research program distinct from (and sometimes critical of) Polanyi's original.
